# Mechanical Energy Conversion

Mechanical energy is the energy possessed by an object due to its motion or its position. The study of how mechanical energy is generated, converted, and transferred is central to [[Physics]] and [[Engineering]]. The conversion of various forms of energy—such as [[Chemical Energy]], [[Heat Energy]], and [[Electrical Energy]]—into usable mechanical work is the fundamental principle behind most modern technology.

## Energy Conversion Principles

The core function of an engine or power source is to facilitate the conversion of one energy form into another, ultimately producing mechanical work.

### 1. Heat Engines (열기관)
A heat engine is a device that converts thermal energy (heat) into mechanical work. This process relies on creating a temperature differential, allowing heat to flow from a high-temperature source to a low-temperature sink.

*   **Internal Combustion Engines (내연기관):** These engines burn fuel (such as gasoline or diesel) within the engine's working chamber. The rapid expansion of the resulting hot, high-pressure gases drives a piston or turbine. Examples include [[Automobile Engine]]s, [[Jet Engine]]s, and [[Rocket Engine]]s.
*   **External Combustion Engines (외연기관):** In these engines, the heat source is external to the working cylinder. The most prominent example is the [[Steam Engine]], where fuel is burned in a separate boiler to create high-pressure steam, which then expands to drive the mechanical components.
    *   **Power Generation:** Large-scale power plants (e.g., [[Thermal Power Plant]]s) operate on this principle. Fuel is burned to create heat, which generates steam, which drives a turbine connected to a [[Generator]].
    *   **Nuclear Power:** While [[Nuclear Reactor]]s use nuclear decay heat rather than combustion, the principle remains similar: heat is generated externally and used to create steam, making the overall system function similarly to an external combustion engine.

### 2. Electrical and Electric Motors
[[Electric Motors]] represent a distinct class of energy conversion device. They convert [[Electrical Energy]] directly into mechanical rotational motion. Unlike heat engines, they do not rely on combustion or thermal expansion as their primary working principle.

### 3. Biological Engines
Life itself utilizes complex energy conversion systems. Muscle contraction is an example of a biological engine. It converts stored [[Chemical Energy]] (specifically, [[Adenosine Triphosphate]] or ATP) into mechanical work by initiating the sliding filament mechanism between actin and myosin proteins.

## Advanced Concepts and Theoretical Models

*   **Stirling Engine:** This is a type of external combustion engine that operates in a closed system, meaning it does not require the continuous intake or expulsion of working material (like air or gas) to function.
*   **Carnot Engine:** The [[Carnot Engine]] is not a physical machine but a theoretical model representing the most efficient possible heat engine operating between two specified temperatures. It serves as a benchmark for the maximum efficiency of any real-world heat engine.

## Energy Flow and Efficiency
Energy conversion processes are rarely 100% efficient. In a typical power generation cycle (e.g., coal $\rightarrow$ heat $\rightarrow$ steam $\rightarrow$ mechanical $\rightarrow$ electricity), significant energy is lost, primarily as waste heat. The efficiency of a system is defined as the ratio of useful output energy to the total input energy.
